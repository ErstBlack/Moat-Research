## Discovery story

`sec_enforcement_structured_corpus` was identified during the 2026-05-05 T1
discovery synthesis pass (iteration `20260505T163223Z-a75c26`) targeting
Lane-4 rebalancing after the L2 cluster reached 9 entries. The SEC
enforcement corpus is the natural financial-regulatory analogue to the
existing FERC eLibrary brief (07.006) — both are large federal regulatory
records where the raw documents are archived but the structured extraction +
entity resolution artifact is the moat.

The key moat thesis: the SEC has published Litigation Releases since 1995
and Administrative Proceedings since the early 1990s. Each case names a
respondent, cites specific statutory violations, and records penalty terms.
Over 30 years, the same individuals and firms appear across multiple
cases — a portfolio manager who settled a 10b-5 insider-trading action in
2002 may have appeared as a relief defendant in 2008 and re-emerged as an
investment adviser respondent in 2019. That 30-year cross-case entity chain
is nowhere preserved in structured form in any free public dataset.

The distinctive aspects of this brief vs. the FERC brief:

1. **Higher source stability.** sec.gov/litigation/ has been continuously
   maintained since 1995 with no documented URL restructuring. FERC eLibrary
   uses an internal servlet proxy that requires endpoint reverse-engineering
   at build time. The 30-year institutional permanence of SEC enforcement
   records materially de-risks the source-stability concern.

2. **Smaller storage footprint.** ~10,000 enforcement actions since 1995 at
   ~200 KB average = ~2 GB raw archive. The FERC eLibrary brief estimated
   ~500 GB raw (10M+ filings). The SEC's enforcement corpus is targeted
   (400–600 new cases/year) rather than comprehensive regulatory filings.

3. **Broader buyer pool.** Securities law enforcement analytics buyers span
   law firms, asset managers, RegTech vendors, fintech background-check
   companies, academic researchers, and investigative journalism —
   materially broader than the FERC brief's energy-specialist buyer base.

## §5 Lane-4 verification

Per CONSTRAINTS §5 and RUBRIC.md Defensibility hard floor:

For Lane-4 briefs, the §5 test is whether the DERIVED ARTIFACT (not the raw
inputs) can be reconstructed from free public sources. The raw enforcement
HTML pages and PDFs ARE publicly archived by SEC — this is expected for
Lane 4 and does not trigger §5. The question is whether the structured
entity graph + violation taxonomy + penalty database exists as a free public
source.

1. **Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse (SCAC)** — confirmed to
   cover private PLAINTIFF class actions only. SEC enforcement actions are
   a distinct universe: federal court civil injunctive cases brought by the
   SEC itself, and administrative proceedings before SEC ALJs. SCAC does not
   index SEC enforcement records. Verified on 2026-05-05.

2. **SEC EDGAR full-text search (EFTS)** — a search interface (efts.sec.gov),
   not a structured database. EFTS returns filing documents but does not
   provide: entity resolution across respondents, violation taxonomy
   extraction, or penalty-amount normalization. It is a prerequisite input
   to our pipeline, not a substitute for the derived artifact.

3. **Bloomberg Law / Westlaw Edge / Lex Machina / Cornerstone Research** —
   commercial at $20k–$150k+/year. Paid commercial does not trigger §5 per
   project precedent (same as PatentsView/PatSnap for USPTO brief, S&P
   Velocity Suite for FERC, Interline for transit-GTFSRT). These alternatives
   demonstrate market existence (pricing precedent) but not free
   reconstructibility.

4. **SEC EDGAR open data API** — provides EDGAR filing metadata (form type,
   filer, date) but does NOT provide enforcement-action structured data
   (respondent entity type, violation statute citations, penalty amounts,
   consent order prohibition terms). The API is an EDGAR filer index, not
   an enforcement corpus.

§5 hard-floor verdict: **passes.** Defensibility=7 (compute-as-barrier, not
temporal-loss). No free public structured cross-case SEC enforcement corpus
with entity resolution exists. The derived artifact is not reconstructible
from free sources.
