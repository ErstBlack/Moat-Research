## Discovery story

`uspto_patent_claim_citation_corpus` was identified during the 2026-05-05 T2
discovery synthesis pass as a Lane-4 candidate explicitly diversifying the
lane cluster away from L2 saturation (5 entries) and complementing the
existing Lane-4 cluster (CSLB, medical-board, FERC, insurance, flood-fusion)
with an IP-vertical brief.

The pattern observation that distinguishes this from a "duplicate the bulk
XML" §5-failure framing:

1. USPTO publishes raw patent grant XML at bulkdata.uspto.gov continuously;
   raw is fully archived and reconstructible by anyone — Lane 1 fails §5.
2. PatentsView (USPTO-funded, free) publishes structured patent data with
   US-only inventor disambiguation and quarterly-cadence citations, BUT
   does NOT publish claim-dependency parse trees, cross-jurisdiction
   inventor ER, or weekly-cadence citation increments.
3. Paid commercial competitors (PatSnap, Derwent, Orbit, Innography) cover
   the full feature set at $50k–$200k+/year — well above the price tier
   accessible to academic labs, boutique IP firms, and startup patent
   counsel.
4. The structurally underserved tier is real, large, and durable: ~250 US
   research universities, several hundred boutique IP firms, and the
   academic innovation-economics research community all need structured
   patent data at PatentsView's feature depth + commercial-incumbent
   feature breadth, and would pay $1k–$25k/year for it.

The Lane-4 thesis claims all three cerebrum defensibility pillars (compute-
as-barrier, ongoing-update compounding, cross-jurisdiction entity resolution)

## §5 Lane-4 verification

Per the cerebrum Lane-4 methodology, §5 verification requires the brief to
claim ≥2 of {compute-as-barrier, ongoing-update compounding, cross-
jurisdiction entity resolution}. This brief claims all three in v1:

1. **Compute-as-barrier — present and strong.** Building the claim-tree
   parser (XPath over USPTO Patent DTD's nested claim elements with proper
   handling of dependency references) + maintaining the incremental
   citation graph + running cross-jurisdiction inventor ER (embedding-based
   name + assignee + technology-class similarity with manual disambiguation
   queue) is genuinely 6–9 months of focused work for a competent solo
   operator. An analyst with the same raw XML needs that same investment;
   the result is a stale one-shot snapshot vs. the live ongoing-update
   product the brief delivers.

2. **Ongoing-update compounding — present and strong.** USPTO publishes
   weekly grant XML; the corpus's current value is strictly tied to
   continuous capture. Citation networks become more analytically valuable
   as more grants link into them (each new grant updates forward-citation
   counts on existing patents within hours of release — a feature
   PatentsView's quarterly refresh structurally cannot match).

3. **Cross-jurisdiction entity resolution — present and v1-delivered.**
   Joining USPTO + EPO PATSTAT + WIPO PCT inventor records via name +
   co-inventor + assignee + technology-class join keys, with manual
   disambiguation for low-confidence matches, is the v1 deliverable, not
   a roadmap. PatentsView's disambiguation covers only the US corpus;
   commercial competitors (PatSnap, Derwent) cover cross-jurisdiction
   ER but at $50k–$200k+/year. The brief's mid-tier price point relies
   on this pillar being the primary differentiation.

§5 hard-floor verdict: **passes** (claims all 3, fully delivered in v1).
Defensibility=7, not 0. Composite 6.911 stands.

The §5 framing here is distinct from a "duplicate the USPTO bulk XML"
thesis (which would fail immediately) — the moat is explicitly NOT raw
data access but derived analytical artifacts that no free public source
publishes at the brief's targeted scope and cadence.
