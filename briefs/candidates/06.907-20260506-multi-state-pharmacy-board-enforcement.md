description: |
  Cross-state entity-resolved corpus of pharmacist, pharmacy technician, and
  pharmacy facility disciplinary actions (license suspensions, revocations,
  probations, consent agreements, voluntary surrenders, criminal-conviction
  notices) across all 50 US state pharmacy boards. Each state board publishes
  enforcement orders independently on its own portal in its own format; there
  is no unified publicly accessible cross-state pharmacy enforcement corpus.
  The National Association of Boards of Pharmacy (NABP) e-Profile Connect is
  subscription-based and restricted to licensed requestors; NABP's public
  disciplinary summaries lack full order text and cross-state entity resolution.
  Unlike physician credentialing (where FSMB publishes a Physician Data Center
  for credentialing organizations), there is no FSMB equivalent for pharmacists

  the one the multi-state medical board brief addresses. The defensible artifact

## Discovery story

`multi_state_pharmacy_board_enforcement` was identified during the 2026-05-06 T1
discovery synthesis pass (iteration `20260506T094308Z-dd2ef2`) as a Lane-4
candidate filling the most significant adjacent gap to the existing
`multi-state-medical-board-enforcement` brief (6.892).

The key observation was structural: the physician credentialing market has the
FSMB Physician Data Center (a restricted-access paid product) as a near-analog,
meaning the `multi-state-medical-board-enforcement` brief competes against an
established commercial incumbent. The pharmacist enforcement market has NO
comparable free or restricted aggregator at the full-text cross-state level —
NABP e-Profile Connect provides per-query lookups for licensed requestors but
no bulk analytics corpus, and NABP's public summaries lack order text. This
makes the free-aggregator gap for pharmacy enforcement structurally larger than
for medical enforcement.

The NPI crosswalk advantage is the primary architectural distinction from the
medical board pattern. Every pharmacist in the US has an NPI (National Provider
Identifier), publicly available from the CMS NPI registry. Using NPI as a
universal cross-state join key dramatically reduces the manual disambiguation
burden in the entity-resolution layer — where the medical board brief relies on
name + DOB + medical-school year as join keys (requiring significant human-in-
the-loop QA), the pharmacy brief can anchor most records to a single government-
issued identifier. This makes the ER layer faster to build, more reliable, and
more defensible (a competitor can't trivially reproduce the NPI-resolved entity
graph without running the same per-state pipeline).

## Rubric calibration note

Composite **6.907** places `multi_state_pharmacy_board_enforcement` slightly
above `multi-state-medical-board-enforcement` (6.892). This is surprising at
first glance since medical boards have a better-established commercial ecosystem,
but is justified by:

- **Lower source stability (5 vs. 6):** Pharmacy portals have higher discovery
  friction (TX/NY portals needed re-verification; SAIS/OSCAR shared platform
  creates simultaneous multi-state churn risk). This penalty on the
  implementation axis partially offsets the financial advantage.

- **Better hardware (7.5 vs. 7.0):** Pharmacy boards publish orders monthly
  (not weekly), so aggregate request rate drops from ~10 req/min (medical) to
  ~1.6 req/min (pharmacy). This pushes concurrency_cost from 8 → 9, raising
  hardware composite 7.0 → 7.5.

- **Better market gap (8 vs. 7):** No FSMB equivalent exists for pharmacists;
  the free-aggregator gap is larger.

- **Similar implementation otherwise:** Solo-operator load 5 vs. 4 (pharmacy is
  slightly easier per-cycle due to monthly vs. weekly cadence + NPI crosswalk
  reducing ER burden). Financial axis 7.0 vs. 7.333 (medical board has a
  larger buyer_existence and more established pricing precedent). The net result
  is essentially a tie (6.907 vs. 6.892), which correctly reflects that these
  two briefs are Lane-4 equivalents in adjacent healthcare-regulatory verticals
  with complementary strengths.

No rubric changes recommended.

## §5 Lane-4 verification

This brief does NOT claim Lane-1 ephemerality. The per-state pharmacy board
enforcement PDFs and HTML pages ARE publicly archived by each state board. The
moat is explicitly a Lane-4 derived-artifact thesis.

**Three-pillar Lane-4 test (cerebrum standard):**

1. **Compute-as-barrier:** OCR + NER + structured extraction from 50 state
   portals with heterogeneous formats (HTML tables, text-layer PDFs, scanned-
   image PDFs, SAIS/OSCAR system exports) across 3-15 page enforcement orders
   requires months of engineering to reproduce at scale. No analyst with the
   raw PDFs can produce an equivalent structured corpus in a weekend.

2. **Ongoing-update compounding:** State boards publish new orders monthly to
   quarterly. The corpus's temporal coverage compounds with each update cycle;
   a 2027 entrant starting fresh cannot recover the full 2026 enforcement history
   with the same monthly temporal granularity (even if they have access to all
   current PDFs, older PDFs may be removed from portals as part of state-board
   record-retention schedules).

3. **Cross-state entity resolution as v1 deliverable:** NPI crosswalk enables
   canonical pharmacist-identity resolution across all 50 state license-number
   namespaces in the v1 build. This is a structural deliverable, not a roadmap
   item. A pharmacist disciplined in CA who moves and re-licenses in TX without
   disclosing the CA action is not surfaced by any per-state query or by NABP's
   restricted per-lookup service; the cross-state NPI-resolved record is the
   unique product.

All three pillars present and v1-deliverable. §5 passes under Lane-4 three-pillar
test; Defensibility=7.
