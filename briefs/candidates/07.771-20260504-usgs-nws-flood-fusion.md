description: |
  Continuously archive USGS NWIS instantaneous discharge, stage, and velocity
  readings from 8,000+ stream gauges (15-minute intervals) and join them against
  NWS Storm Events polygon records (obtained as bulk CSV from NCEI) to produce
  the storm-impact gauge-response dataset: for every declared storm event, which
  gauges were inside the polygon, what was the peak discharge, the time-to-peak
  lag from storm onset, and the shape of the recession curve.

  Neither source is ephemeral — USGS archives its gauge data indefinitely and
  NCEI archives Storm Events records. The moat is the TIME-ALIGNED JOIN with
  derived sub-event features (time-to-peak, peak discharge relative to 10-year
  flood threshold, recession curve coefficient) that nobody publishes as a
  clean machine-readable dataset at national coverage with sub-daily granularity.
  The historical depth of this fusion — covering every storm event since gauge
  installation across thousands of gauges — is practically irreproducible once
  the join-key precision (15-minute gauge readings correlated to storm polygon
  onset/offset times) requires going back to verify whether earlier archives
  preserved the right fields. As more storms pass, the fusion archive compounds;
  each new storm event adds a data point that cannot be retroactively inserted
  into a competing archive started later.

  The lane-4 secondary angle: raw gauge records and storm polygons are public,
  but the JOIN at scale across 8,000+ stations across decades of storm events
  is a substantial compute task. A pre-computed, continuously-updated fusion
  artifact that research teams can query directly is the monetizable product;
  compute is the barrier to casual reproduction.

## Discovery story

`usgs_nws_flood_fusion` was identified by the operator during the initial
WISHLIST seeding pass on 2026-05-04 as the first Lane 3 (cross-source fusion)
candidate in the corpus. The observation was structural:

1. USGS NWIS maintains 8,000+ stream gauges reporting discharge, stage, and
   velocity at 15-minute intervals. The raw data is archived indefinitely at
   `waterdata.usgs.gov/nwis/iv`. The source is not ephemeral.
2. NWS Storm Events Database records every declared storm event with polygon
   geometry and onset/offset times, archived at NCEI as bulk CSV downloads.
   The source is not ephemeral.
3. The JOIN of these two datasets — every gauge response during every storm
   event, with sub-event features (time-to-peak, peak discharge, recession
   curve) — **does not exist as a machine-readable public dataset at national
   coverage**. The fusion is the moat, not the sources.

The WISHLIST entry flagged one ambiguity: `api.weather.gov/robots.txt` has
`Disallow: /` for `User-agent: *`, which is technically a robots exclusion
that would cover scripted access. The WISHLIST analysis noted this is
"aimed at search engine indexers" and that the NCEI bulk CSV path "may have
cleaner ToS; brief should evaluate both access paths."

This brief resolves the ambiguity by **pivoting to the NCEI bulk CSV path**:
NCEI (National Centers for Environmental Information) is the explicitly-designed
research archive for NWS Storm Events data, provides bulk CSV downloads
intended for programmatic research use, and is US government public domain.
The `api.weather.gov` endpoint is not used. The `legal_tos_risk` sub-criterion
reflects one outstanding item: NCEI robots.txt must be verified before
promoting this brief to `approved`.

## Fusion artifact moat thesis (defensibility justification)

The standard objection to Lane 3 candidates is: "If both sources are archived,
can't anyone compute this join later?" For `usgs_nws_flood_fusion`, the answer
is technically yes but practically constrained in ways that create real
defensibility:

1. **Polygon geometry drift**: NWS revises storm event polygon geometries
   retroactively as better observational data is incorporated. A join computed
   at event time captures the polygon as-declared; a join computed 5 years
   later uses the revised geometry. These differ, and for events near polygon
   boundaries, gauge inclusion/exclusion changes. The "correct" join for
   flood insurance purposes is the as-declared geometry; only an operator
   who computed the join contemporaneously has it.

2. **NWIS instantaneous value availability**: USGS does not guarantee
   indefinite sub-daily availability for all stations in its historical
   archive. Station file formats have evolved; early digital records have
   QA flags that differ from modern records. A join computed now using
   the current API response is cleaner than one computed retroactively
   against an imperfect historical export.

3. **Derived feature precision**: time-to-peak, recession curve coefficient,
   and flood threshold ratio are computed from the raw 15-minute gauge
   series within the storm window. These are non-trivial to reproduce at
   scale (spatial join + time-window extraction + curve fitting across 8,000
   stations × thousands of storm events). A competitor starting later must
   spend the compute to reproduce them; we have them pre-computed and
   continuously updated.

4. **Archive depth compounds**: each storm season adds ~50-100 new NCEI
   Storm Events records. Our archive's value increases with depth (trend
   analysis, return-period estimation). A competitor starting 2 years later
   is permanently 2 years behind in historical depth, which matters for
   users who need long-period return estimates (10-year, 25-year, 100-year
   flood thresholds require decades of storm-response data to calibrate).

**No rubric changes recommended.** The three-axis formula correctly
