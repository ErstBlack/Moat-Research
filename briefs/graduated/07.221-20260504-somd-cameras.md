## Discovery story

`somd-cameras` is the archetype this project was built to find more of, not a
moat that `moat-research` discovered. The operator started building it well
before the rubric existed, after noticing two facts at the same time:

1. MDOT CHART publishes high-quality public HLS streams from dozens of cameras
   along Maryland's southern corridors, and
2. nobody — including MDOT — retains the footage. CHART is a real-time incident
   coordination tool for the agency; the cameras are wired straight through to
   a public viewer with no archival side-channel.

That asymmetry (live for everyone, history for nobody) is the textbook lane-1
moat: an ephemeral public dataset where the only entity that can ever own the
historical record is the first operator who decided to record it. Everything
else in the project — the CV stack, the RWIS cross-validator, the weather
classifier, the anomaly detector, the parquet cold tier — is downstream
amplification of that single observation.

This brief exists to formalize what was previously implicit: to score
`somd-cameras` against the rubric the moat hunter is now using, and to anchor
the corpus with a worked example that future briefs are pattern-matched
against. It is filed as `graduated` because the project is real and operating;
the rescore is post-hoc validation, not a go/no-go gate.

## Rubric calibration note

A composite of **7.221** is in the right neighborhood but feels modestly
*low* for the canonical reference moat. Operator intuition would put
`somd-cameras` in the 7.5–8.5 band, because:

- The defensibility-10 (past unrecoverable) is the strongest form the rubric
  defines and is doing real work here, but it gets averaged into a financial
  axis where buyer-existence and pricing-precedent are appropriately
  discounted for the SOMD-niche geography. The geometric weighting (financial
  at 0.4) further discounts the strongest-feature axis.
- The implementation-axis 7.25 is conservative on `legal_tos_risk` (8 because
  a future ToS change is possible) and `source_stability` (7 because of
  observed roster drift). Both are honest scores, but together they pull a
  pure-public-feed moat below 8 on an axis where it should arguably score
  higher.
- The hardware axis is 8 mostly because storage_growth_rate is 8 (90 GB/month
  is real, not free) and concurrency_cost is 7 (35 HLS fetches against a
  single host is non-trivial). That's also honest.

Net: the score is well-calibrated *internally*, and the discount comes from
the niche geography (financial) plus realistic operational costs
(implementation, hardware) — not from the rubric being miscalibrated. The
defensibility-10 alone cannot, and arguably should not, drag a small-market
moat into the 9+ band.

**No rubric edits are recommended at this time.** If, after Item 3 of FOCUS.md
(`WISHLIST.md` seeding) and a few more candidates have been scored, every
serious lane-1 moat is clustering in the low 7s while operator intuition keeps
ranking some of them as clearly stronger than others, the calibration to
revisit first would be the **financial-axis weight** (currently 0.4 vs. 0.3 /
0.3): a moat whose distinguishing feature is the unrecoverability of the past
deserves *more* weight on the dimension that captures that fact, which is
defensibility (financial-axis sub-criterion). Adjusting weights *up* on
defensibility within the financial sub-average — or splitting defensibility
out into its own top-level axis — would be the surgical change. Do not act on
this until at least three more lane-1 candidates have been scored.
