## Discovery story

`ndbc_realtime_buoys` was identified by the operator during the initial WISHLIST
seeding pass on 2026-05-04 as the first non-cameras candidate in Lane 1. The
observation pattern mirrors `somd-cameras` almost exactly:

1. NOAA NDBC publishes high-quality real-time oceanographic data from ~1,000
   buoys globally, updated every 10-50 minutes, via a publicly accessible HTTP
   directory at `https://www.ndbc.noaa.gov/data/realtime2/`.
2. The spectral wave density files (w-files, e.g. `41025w.txt`) report wave
   height broken out by frequency bin -- the kind of data that matters for
   offshore operations planning and marine insurance -- but each file is
   **overwritten** as new observations arrive.
3. NCEI appears to archive the primary hourly-aggregated observations but NOT
   the full-resolution spectral data at sub-hourly cadence. If accurate, this
   means every 10-50 minute spectral reading that passes without capture is
   gone permanently.

The moat candidate was added to `WISHLIST.md` with a flag: "Brief must verify
NCEI archive completeness for spectral (w-file) data before asserting Lane 1 --
if NCEI archives spectral files fully, the ephemeral argument weakens." That
flag is reflected in `disqualifiers_checked.ncei_spectral_archival_unverified`
and drives the conservative scores on `market_gap` (6) and `defensibility` (9
rather than 10).

The hardware and implementation profile is substantially better than
`somd-cameras`: 1 GB/month vs. 90 GB/month storage, no GPU required, no
HLS/video complexity, no inference pipeline. The operational footprint is about
as small as a moat project can get.

## Verification outcome (2026-05-04 — rejection)

The flagged action item ("verify NCEI spectral (w-file) archive completeness")
was executed 2026-05-04 with a slightly broader scope: not just NCEI, but every
plausible public archive path for spectral wave density. The result reverses
the brief's working assumption.

**What was verified.** Three nested public archive tiers on NDBC's own
infrastructure, all at the same :10/:40 sub-hourly cadence as the realtime
files:

1. `/data/realtime2/<station>.spec` — current observations, overwritten on each
   poll (this is the file the brief proposed to capture).
2. `/data/swden/{Jan|Feb|...}/<station><MM><YYYY>.txt.gz` — monthly
   intermediate archive. Spot-check `4100212026.txt.gz` (January 2026, station
   41002) shows rows at `2026 01 01 00 10` and `2026 01 01 00 40` etc — the
   identical sub-hourly cadence preserved.
3. `/data/historical/swden/<station>w<YYYY>.txt.gz` — annual rollup. Spot-check
   `41001w2025.txt.gz` last-modified 2026-02-11; rows at :10/:40.

The realtime "10–50 minute" cadence cited in the original brief description
was speculative — actual NDBC observation cycle is twice-hourly at fixed
minutes (:10 and :40), and that exact cadence is preserved in both the monthly
and annual archives. There is no temporal-resolution gap between what the
brief proposed to capture and what NDBC publishes for free.

**Why this triggers rejection.** Per `CONSTRAINTS.md` §5 and the `RUBRIC.md`
Defensibility hard floor (both added 2026-05-04 in the calibration pass), if
the dataset can be reconstructed later by any analyst from currently-public
archived sources, Defensibility=0 → financial axis=0 → composite=0 →
auto-reject. The NDBC swden archive is exactly that: any analyst can
reconstruct a 30-minute spectral history of any station at any time from
publicly-archived files. The moat thesis collapses.

**Why a Lane-3/Lane-4 reframe was considered and dismissed.** The natural
reframe is "the join with NWS storm events / vessel transits / etc. is the
moat" (Lane 3) or "spectral feature engineering at scale is the moat" (Lane
4). Both fail by parity with the same calibration cycle that re-categorized
the USGS×NWS flood fusion brief: if the raw sub-hourly inputs are publicly
archived (they are), a Lane-3 join is reconstructible by anyone. A Lane-4
derived-artifact thesis (e.g., a transformer-based wave-spectrum model) is
plausible *in principle*, but compute cost is the only barrier and the brief
has not articulated a derived artifact specific enough to score; reframing now
would be wishful, not analytical. If the operator later identifies a concrete
Lane-4 derived artifact whose value is the compute, that's a fresh wishlist
entry, not a revival of this brief.

**Score reset.** Defensibility 9 → 0; financial composite 6.167 → 0.000 (axis
forced 0 by hard floor); overall composite 7.600 → 0.000. Status: scored →
rejected. The implementation (8.25) and hardware (9.25) sub-axis numbers are
preserved as-is in the frontmatter for audit/calibration purposes — the
ingestor design itself was sound, only the moat thesis was wrong.

## Original rubric calibration note (frozen for audit; reasoning superseded by the 2026-05-04 verification above)

A composite of **7.600** places `ndbc_realtime_buoys` modestly above
`somd-cameras` (7.221) despite the lane-1 premise being unverified. This
feels like the right ordering: the hardware and implementation profiles are
clearly superior (9.25 and 8.25 vs. 8.0 and 7.25), and those two axes pull
the composite up even though the financial axis is nearly identical (6.167

NCEI archives w-files fully, this becomes a Lane 3 brief; the defensibility
score drops, financial likely falls to 5.5-6.0, and the composite would
settle around 6.5-7.0 (still viable, but weaker).

**Action item for next iteration:** Verify NCEI spectral (w-file) archive
completeness at `https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/search/data-search/global-marine`

**No rubric changes recommended.** The formula is working correctly: it
correctly discounts an unverified lane-1 claim through conservative
`market_gap` scoring rather than through rubric tinkering. The
implementation and hardware axes validating the operational feasibility
independently of the lane-1 premise gives a reliable floor score.
